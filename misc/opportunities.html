Tissue Grafting

The musculoskeletal system is the key part of our body that allows for movement. It is made up of our bones, cartilage, ligaments, tendons, muscles and the connective tissue that binds them all together. Usually damage to this system is quite drastic and the healing process can take months, years or may never happen at all. In recent decades robots have been designed o more accurately replicate this musculoskeletal system. There has been a lot of development into the the growth of of musculoskeletal tissue to be used for grafting onto a human body which would be huge for medicine and science. However Mouthuy and Carr state

“The main limitation of current bioreactors is that they poorly mimic the mechanical loadings that are experienced in the body. To produce clinically relevant grafts, future bioreactors will need to: - provide multidirectional stresses by a combination of tension, torsion, compression and shear stresses - adapt the loading regime to each individual tissue in consideration of their anatomical location - enable the fabrication of tissue constructs with dimensions similar to their native counterparts.”

This is where the robotics come in. By adding this tissue to a robot it will be able to replicate the constant stress, and strain that a human body would constantly be experiencing. This conditioned tissue could be tailor made to better fit patients. This has the potential to be very beneficial to the medical field and can only be achieved through with the help of robotics as it is a mix of some of the most complex aspects of both hardware and software.
